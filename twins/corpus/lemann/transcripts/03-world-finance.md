# The Rise and Rise of Jorge Paulo Lemann

**Source:** World Finance | Elizabeth Matsangou

## Profile
- Brazil's richest man: $26.3bn net worth (self-made)
- Born in Rio de Janeiro to Swiss father
- Part of "The Three Musketeers" with Marcel Herrmann Telles ($13bn) and Carlos Alberto Sicupira ($11.3bn)

## Key Holdings (3G Capital)
- **Anheuser-Busch InBev** (Budweiser, Corona, Stella Artois, Beck's, Hoegaarden, Leffe)
- **Burger King** (acquired 2010, $3.3bn)
- **Heinz** (joint venture with Berkshire Hathaway, 2013)
- **Kraft Heinz** (merged 2015)
- 25% of global beer market

## Early Life & Career

### Harvard (1958-1961)
- Accepted to study economics — rare for a young Brazilian
- Completed in 3 years instead of 4
- Felt he didn't fully appreciate it at the time

### Sports
- Five-time national tennis champion
- Represented Brazil and Switzerland in Davis Cup (dual citizenship)
- Competed at Wimbledon
- Roger Federer is now an investor in 3G Capital

### Banco Garantia (1971-1998)
**"Brazil's Goldman Sachs"**

**Revolutionary Management:**
- Meritocracy-based culture (unprecedented in Brazil)
- Open-plan offices
- Ban on collectibles in workplace
- Elimination of company perks
- Bonus structure: top performers purchase shares
- Rewards based on individual achievement, not seniority

**Hiring: "PSDs"** — Poor, Smart, Deep desire to get rich
- Carlos Alberto Sicupira joined at age 17
- Candidates faced intimidating interviews by 8-10 partners

**Learning Strategy:** Send associates to study competitors
- Interned at Goldman Sachs
- Sam Walton personally invited them to Walmart in Oklahoma

**Impact:** "In Brazil we can talk about banking in two great eras, BL [Before Lemann] and AL [After Lemann]"

**Sold to Credit Suisse in 1998 for $675 million** (after Asian financial crisis)

### AmBev (1999)
- Created from collected breweries (Brahma acquired 1989)
- **Zero-based budgeting** — managers justify all expenditure from zero annually
- Voted "most admired company" in 60 years of Brazilian business
- 2004: Merged with InterBrew ($11bn)
- Later merged with Anheuser-Busch → AB InBev

### 3G Capital (founded 1999)
**Unique Model:**
- Raises capital from close-knit circle of super-rich families
- Investors: Santo Domingo family, Reimann family, Roger Federer, William Ackman
- Buy-and-hold approach
- Obsession with efficiency and productivity

## The "Lemann Touch" — Operational Transformation

### Burger King Case Study
- Appointed Bernardo Hees as CEO (age 41)
- Cut HQ staff from 600 to ~70
- Required executives to fly coach and stay in budget hotels
- CEO answered customer complaints personally via Twitter

### Heinz Transformation
- Replaced 11 of 12 top executives within 2 years
- Required employees to seek approval for color photocopies
- Closed corporate air fleet
- Result: $1.4bn cost reduction in 18 months

### Kraft Heinz
- Post-merger: cut 5,000 jobs
- Margin target: 30% (from 18%)
- Predictable playbook: aggressive cuts + 3G management system

## Core Management Philosophy

### Meritocracy Above All
- Best people crave it; mediocre people fear it
- Performance > seniority, credentials, or relationships

### Zero-Based Budgeting
- Every expense re-justified annually
- Target: always lower than prior year

### Ownership Mentality
- "We want partners, not merely shareholders"
- Variable compensation tied to company performance

### Talent Obsession
- "Could you imagine a sports coach outsourcing selection of his first XI?"
- Homegrown talent through internship pipeline
- CEOs promoted from within: Carlos Brito (AB InBev), Bernardo Hees (Burger King/Heinz)

### Continuous Learning
- Even after 15 years at Garantia, Telles and Sicupira completed Harvard's OPM program
- "If these 3 can find time, anyone can find time"

## Personal Philosophy
- **Risk:** "I consider risk a part of life, and to know how to take risks you need to train"
- **Difficulty:** "Difficulty generates the need to improve"
- **Resilience:** "You cannot lose heart at the first difficulty. Those who continue will do well."
- **Surfing metaphor:** The Copacabana wave taught more about risk than Harvard
